# Peril Scoring Rubric — v1

*The scoring "brain" for the Political Risk Terminal. Each peril is scored 1–10 against the anchored bands below. Draft v1 — refine with the desk (Nick) once the internship starts.*

---

## How to score (rules that apply to every peril)

1. **Score the country/peril on a 1–10 scale** using the bands below.
2. **Always also state direction**: `escalating`, `stable`, or `easing` — based on whether the recent news suggests the situation is getting worse, holding, or improving.
3. **Bind every score to evidence.** A score must be justified by specific headline(s). No evidence → score it low and flag low confidence.
4. **State confidence** (`high` / `medium` / `low`) based on how much clear, relevant news there is.
5. **Score the signal, not the noise.** A burst of coverage is not the same as a worsening situation. Weight what *happened* (a decree, a default, troops deployed) over how loudly it was reported.
6. **Change matters more than level.** A country that is chronically tense but stable is less actionable than one accelerating from calm. The direction field carries this.

## The five perils

### 1. Expropriation & CEND
*Confiscation, Expropriation, Nationalisation, Deprivation — government action that strips an investor of ownership, control, or use of assets.*

- **1–2 (Benign):** Stable property rights; no hostile rhetoric or policy signals toward foreign/private assets.
- **3–4 (Low / watch):** Occasional political rhetoric against foreign ownership; isolated regulatory friction; nothing concrete.
- **5–6 (Elevated):** Credible policy proposals touching ownership (forced localisation, licence reviews, "strategic sector" talk); a sector-specific dispute emerging.
- **7–8 (High):** Active measures underway — asset seizures, forced divestment, licence revocations, or nationalisation legislation in progress.
- **9–10 (Severe):** Broad or completed expropriation/nationalisation; assets seized; investors locked out.

### 2. Political Violence
*War, civil war, insurrection, terrorism, sabotage, coup — physical violence that threatens people, operations, or assets.*

- **1–2 (Benign):** Peaceful; no organised violence or credible threat.
- **3–4 (Low / watch):** Isolated incidents; localised tension; elevated but non-specific terrorism chatter.
- **5–6 (Elevated):** Recurring violence in parts of the country; militant activity; coup rumours; border skirmishes.
- **7–8 (High):** Sustained armed conflict, insurgency, terrorism campaign, or attempted/successful coup affecting stability.
- **9–10 (Severe):** Full-scale war or state collapse; widespread, ongoing violence.

### 3. Currency Inconvertibility & Transfer Risk
*Inability to convert local currency to hard currency or to transfer funds out of the country.*

- **1–2 (Benign):** Free convertibility; stable FX reserves; no capital controls.
- **3–4 (Low / watch):** Mild FX pressure; minor administrative delays on transfers; reserves slipping.
- **5–6 (Elevated):** Reserves under clear strain; new or tightened capital controls discussed/introduced; widening parallel exchange rate.
- **7–8 (High):** Active restrictions on conversion/repatriation; significant transfer delays; sharp reserve depletion.
- **9–10 (Severe):** Convertibility effectively frozen; funds trapped; FX market non-functional.

### 4. Sovereign Default & Non-Payment
*Failure of a sovereign (or state entity) to honour debt or contractual financial obligations — "non-honouring of sovereign obligations."*

- **1–2 (Benign):** Servicing debt normally; stable ratings; market access intact.
- **3–4 (Low / watch):** Rising debt-service stress; negative ratings commentary; spreads widening modestly.
- **5–6 (Elevated):** IMF talks; restructuring discussions; ratings downgrades into speculative territory; missed minor payments.
- **7–8 (High):** Imminent or selective default; arrears accumulating; restructuring being forced.
- **9–10 (Severe):** Hard default / repudiation; broad non-payment; loss of market access.

### 5. Civil Unrest (SRCC)
*Strikes, riots, civil commotion — large-scale public disorder short of organised armed conflict.*

- **1–2 (Benign):** Calm; no significant protest activity.
- **3–4 (Low / watch):** Routine, peaceful protests or limited strikes; manageable grievances.
- **5–6 (Elevated):** Large or spreading protests; significant strikes; sporadic clashes with police; rising public anger.
- **7–8 (High):** Sustained nationwide unrest; violent clashes; general strikes; curfews or states of emergency.
- **9–10 (Severe):** Mass uprising / revolution-scale disorder; government control contested in the streets.
